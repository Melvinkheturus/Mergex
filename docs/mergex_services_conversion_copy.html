The conversion psychology behind every copy decision - share this with your team so future copy stays consistent.
Rule 1 - Pain before solution
Every card opens with the client's pain in italics - not a description of what Mergex does. Humans decide emotionally before they decide rationally. The pain hook earns the right to explain the solution. Without it, the card is just a service list.
Rule 2 - Quote format for pain hooks
The pain hooks are written as if the client is speaking - in quotation marks, first person. This is deliberate. When a visitor reads their own internal monologue reflected back at them, they feel understood. That feeling is the conversion trigger - not the feature list below it.
Rule 3 - Scale and Persona are elevated
Scale (Systems) and Persona (Labs) span the full width with premium card styling. This tells the eye they matter before the brain reads a word. Elevated visual hierarchy creates perceived value - visitors assume the highlighted card is the most important offering, which positions both correctly.
Rule 4 - Entry framing changes everything
The entry framing ("Most businesses need all of this. Most are paying five vendors to get it.") is placed before the visitor sees any group. This single paragraph changes how they read every card below - from "interesting service" to "I need this and I've been doing it wrong." Without it, the groups are a menu. With it, they're a revelation.
Rule 5 - "This is where most agencies stop"
The Scale card description includes: "This is where most agencies stop and where Mergex starts." This is a direct competitive positioning statement that doesn't name any competitor. It works because every visitor already knows agencies stop at delivery - Manikandan's insight about the gap between strategy and execution is now embedded in the service copy itself.
